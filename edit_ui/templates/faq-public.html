Frequently Asked Questions

What is Maptcha?

Maptcha is a web-based interface doing rough placements of historical maps on modern geography.

The first version of maptcha.org was launched by Mike Migurski in July 2011, using the 1905 Sanborn Fire Insurance Atlas as its first map series test case. The atlas contained about 700 pages of street level maps of San Francisco, and when it was launched, volunteers from around the world used the site to place each page on to a modern map of the city, all within a few days, which was a wonderful surprise!

The application was further developed in 2012 by Stamen Design in San Francisco, under a grant from TheÂ Bill Lane Center for the American West at Stanford University to support for the 2013Year of the Bay project, crowdsourcing a new history of the San Francisco Bay Area.

How do I get started?

When you open up the tool, you'll see two panels. The left panel contains the old map, and the right panel shows you a modern map of the same (or similar area). The goal is to use the rotation and scaling widget (the big yellow circle thingy on the left) to line up the old map on top of the new. You can drag and drop the maps around, and zoom in and out of both. You can also search for a specific place on the modern map to jump to a place you recognize on the old map.

You might be working on a map that's part of an atlas. Maptcha will let you place as many maps as you have time to do, so, once you're happy with the arrangement, click on the FINISHED button and move to the next map.

It's possible you'll come up against a map that's not possible to place for some reason, like, the page is too damaged to read properly, or there aren't any features on the old map that you can recognize on a modern map, or it's simply too tricky. If that happens, you can click on the SKIP THIS MAP button to move on to a new map.

You can stop at any time, of course.

I have some old maps I'd like to add to Maptcha. How do I do that?

Please get in touch with us. We'd love to find new institutional partners with map collections who are interested to have volunteers place historical maps on modern geography. In fact, we'd love to keep enhancing the Maptcha application, so would love to find potential grant partners to help fund that development work.

Is this open source? Can I use it at my institution?

Not yet, but we'd love to help you try. Right now, the code is up on Github, and we'd love to open it up to the world. Your demand will help move this forward, so please let us know you're interested.
